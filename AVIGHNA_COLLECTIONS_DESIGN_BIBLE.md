# PART I — BRAND FOUNDATION

---

## 1. Brand Vision

**To make every Indian woman feel like royalty — every single day.**

Avighna Collections envisions a world where the elegance of fine jewellery is not locked behind extraordinary price tags. We exist to democratise luxury — to bring the weight, warmth, and radiance of gold to women who deserve to feel precious, celebrated, and adorned.

Our digital flagship store is not a shop. It is a sanctuary of beauty.

---

## 2. Brand Mission

To curate the finest 1 gram gold jewellery and fashion accessories in India — presented with the reverence, craftsmanship, and editorial grace of the world's greatest jewellery houses — and delivered with the intimacy of a personal jeweller who knows your name.

**We do not sell jewellery. We create moments of joy.**

## 3. Brand Values

### 3.1 Craftsmanship
Every piece in our collection is chosen for its artistry. We celebrate the hands that make beauty possible. Our digital experience must reflect the same level of care and intention that goes into every piece we curate.

### 3.2 Accessibility with Dignity
We make luxury accessible — but we never make it feel cheap. The woman who spends ₹1,500 on a necklace from Avighna deserves to feel exactly as celebrated as the woman who spends ₹1,50,000 at a fine jewellery counter.

### 3.3 Authenticity
We do not pretend. We do not inflate. We present our jewellery honestly, beautifully, and with pride. Every piece is real. Every claim is honest. Every experience is genuine.

### 3.4 Feminine Power
Our brand celebrates the modern Indian woman in all her complexity — traditional and contemporary, professional and celebratory, minimalist and maximalist. She contains multitudes. So do we.

### 3.5 Emotional Connection
Jewellery is never just an accessory. It is a memory. A milestone. A gift from a mother. A reward for achievement. Our brand understands the emotional weight of what we sell and honours it in every touchpoint.

### 3.6 Restraint
We will always do less to say more. We resist the temptation to fill every space. We trust silence. We trust white space. We trust that the product is enough.

## 7. User Experience Philosophy

### The Showroom Principle
Every page must feel like entering a physical showroom. The customer should feel welcomed, unhurried, and guided — never pushed, never rushed, never overwhelmed.

### The Three Seconds Rule
Within the first three seconds of landing on any page, the customer must feel: *"This is a premium brand I can trust."* If any element undermines this within the first viewport — it must be removed.

### The Editorial Principle
We do not list products. We present collections. The difference is everything. A product listing is a catalogue. An editorial is a story. We tell stories.

### The Silence Principle
White space is not empty. White space is a decision. It says: *"We are confident enough to give you room to breathe."* Every section must have more space than feels comfortable — then add 20% more.

### The Single Focus Principle
Every page has one primary goal. One emotion we want the customer to feel. One action we want them to take. Never two. Never three. One.

### The Delight Protocol
Every interaction should contain at least one moment of unexpected beauty. A hover that reveals a second image. A cart animation that feels alive. A transition so smooth it's almost invisible. Delight is not optional — it is the standard.

---

## 8. Luxury Design Principles

### 8.1 Restraint Over Decoration
Remove everything that does not serve a clear purpose. If you cannot articulate why an element exists — remove it.

### 8.2 Typography as Hierarchy
Typography must communicate importance before the reader reads a single word. Scale, weight, tracking, and line height must create an unmistakable visual hierarchy.

### 8.3 Product is the Hero
Every layout decision must direct the eye toward the product. No decorative element should compete with the product for attention.

### 8.4 Consistency as Trust
Inconsistency signals carelessness. Carelessness signals cheap. Every spacing value, every color application, every animation timing must be drawn from the design system — never improvised.

### 8.5 Motion Must Earn Its Place
Animation that exists for its own sake is noise. Every animation must serve a purpose: to guide attention, to provide feedback, to create delight, to signal quality. All other animation must be removed.

### 8.6 Mobile is Not a Reduction
The mobile experience is not a simplified version of the desktop. It is a fully realised, separately designed experience that happens to share the same product catalogue.

## 9. Brand Voice

### Tone
Warm. Precise. Confident. Editorial. Never corporate. Never salesy.

### Voice Characteristics
- **Speaks to her as an equal**, never down to her, never sycophantically up
- **Uses active, present-tense language** — *"Discover"* not *"Click here to see"*
- **Celebrates beauty without hyperbole** — *"Crafted in gold-layered brass"* not *"AMAZING GOLD JEWELLERY!!!"*
- **Names the feeling, not just the product** — *"Made for your most important day"* not just *"Bridal Collection"*

### Writing Rules
1. Never use exclamation marks in product descriptions
2. Never use the word "cheap" or "affordable" — use "accessible" or "thoughtfully priced"
3. Never write in all capitals except for section labels (and even then, with restraint)
4. Use letter-spacing on labels: `BRIDAL COLLECTION` not `Bridal Collection` for category markers
5. Keep product descriptions poetic but precise
6. Use "discover" not "shop," "explore" not "browse," "curate" not "buy"

### Voice Examples

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---|---|
| "Buy Now" | "Add to Collection" |
| "Sale — 30% Off!" | "Specially Curated" |
| "Free Shipping!" | "Complimentary Delivery" |
| "Limited Stock!" | "Final Pieces" |
| "Check out these amazing products" | "Discover the collection" |
| "Great deals on gold jewellery" | "Jewellery worthy of every occasion" |

## 10. Visual Identity System

### 10.1 Logo Philosophy
The Avighna Collections wordmark must embody restraint and precision. If a custom SVG logotype exists, it is sacred — it must not be altered, recolored, or repositioned without explicit brand approval.

**Logo Usage Rules:**
- Always maintain clear space equal to the cap-height of the "A" on all four sides
- Never stretch, skew, or rotate the logo
- Never apply gradients, shadows, or effects to the logo
- The logo appears in: **Pure Warm White** (on dark backgrounds) or **Deep Charcoal** (on light backgrounds)
- The logo NEVER appears in gold — gold is for accents, not identity
- Minimum size: 120px wide on desktop, 90px wide on mobile

## 12. Typography System

### 12.1 The Font Family

#### Display Typeface — Cormorant Garamond
```
Purpose: All headings, product names, section titles, editorial headlines
Weights: Light (300), Regular (400), Medium (500), SemiBold (600), Bold (700)

Google Fonts: https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Cormorant+Garamond
```

*Cormorant Garamond carries centuries of typographic heritage. It is the typographic voice of fine craftsmanship. It is organic, elegant, and deeply human.*

*DM Sans provides clean, modern legibility without the coldness of Inter or the genericness of Roboto. It pairs perfectly with Cormorant Garamond — organic meets precision.*

```
Purpose: Section labels ("THE COLLECTION"), category markers, navigation sub-labels
```

### 12.3 Typography Rules

1. **Cormorant Garamond is for display only** — never for body text, UI, or buttons
2. **DM Sans is for everything functional** — navigation, buttons, prices, descriptions
3. **Letter-spacing on display text: -0.02em** — tighten headlines for impact
4. **Letter-spacing on labels: +0.25em to +0.40em** — uppercase labels must breathe
5. **Never bold a heading above 56px** — at large sizes, lightness reads as confidence
6. **Italic Cormorant is reserved** for pull quotes, editorial emphasis, and brand moments
7. **Line height on hero text: 1.0 to 1.05** — tight leading on large display type
8. **Line height on body text: 1.65 to 1.75** — generous leading for readability
9. **No underlines on body text links** — use color change (#C9A96E) on hover
10. **Never center-align body text beyond two lines**
11. **Maximum line length: 65 characters** for comfortable reading

## 13. Layout Philosophy

### The Editorial Grid Principle
Avighna does not use standard e-commerce grid layouts. Every page is designed as an editorial layout — some sections full-width, some asymmetrical, some split into unequal columns. Predictable grids are the enemy of luxury.

### The Asymmetry Principle
Luxury layouts break the grid intentionally. A product image that bleeds off the edge. Text that sits in the left third of the page. A headline that starts mid-grid. These decisions signal confidence and artistic direction.

### The Whitespace Standard
Luxury needs room to breathe. Standard sections have padding. Luxury sections have generous padding PLUS additional breathing room.

## 17. Photography Direction

### 17.1 The Image Standard
Every image on the Avighna website communicates the value of the jewellery. Poor photography destroys luxury positioning. Images must be treated as editorial assets, not product catalogue shots.

### 17.2 Photography Style
**Lighting:** Warm, directional light that catches the gold. Never flat studio lighting. Never harsh flashes. The light should behave as if it is early morning sunlight or soft candlelight — warm, golden, intimate.

**Background:** Off-white, warm ivory, pale parchment, or matte champagne linen textures. Never pure white digital backgrounds. Never grey studio backgrounds.

**Models:** When using models — they must be styled with minimal makeup and hair that complements the jewellery without competing with it. Clothing must be muted (cream, white, soft blush) so the jewellery reads first.

**Composition:** Allow breathing room. The jewellery is rarely centred — it sits slightly off-centre, in the upper or lower third, with meaningful negative space around it.

**Detail shots:** Macro photography of the jewellery texture, clasp, setting, and finish are required for every product. These close-up shots communicate craftsmanship.

**Lifestyle shots:** Jewellery shown being worn in natural settings — a puja, a celebration table, a morning window seat. Never posed or awkward.

### 17.3 Color Grading
All images must be color-graded to match the Avighna palette:
- Warm highlights (shift toward champagne gold)
- Lifted shadows (never pure black shadows)
- Slightly desaturated backgrounds
- Rich but not oversaturated gold tones

### 17.4 Image Ratios
- Hero images: `16:9` (desktop), `9:16` (mobile)
- Product cards: `3:4` (portrait)
- Editorial sections: `1:1` or `4:3`
- Category banners: `21:9`
- Detail shots: `1:1`

### 17.5 What is Forbidden in Photography
- Stock photography with generic poses
- Overly digital, retouched skin
- Jewellery on props that don't match the brand aesthetic (plastic, cheap fabric)
- Images with visible pricing tags or other branding
- Pure white or grey studio backgrounds
- Overexposed, washed-out images
- Heavy Photoshop effects, HDR effects, or filters

## 18. Art Direction

### 18.1 The Editorial Principle
Every page layout is designed as a magazine spread, not a product grid. Sections are compositions — the relationship between text, image, space, and product creates the editorial effect.

### 18.2 Compositional Vocabulary

**Split Editorial** — 60/40 image left, text right. Text is large scale, single line, deeply tracked. Below it, a short body paragraph and a thin-bordered CTA.

**Full Bleed with Text Overlay** — A high-quality editorial image fills the entire viewport width. Text appears in the lower-left corner with generous padding, never centred.

**Asymmetric Grid** — A large image takes 7 of 12 columns. A vertically stacked set of smaller elements takes the remaining 5. They do not align to a common top — the image is taller.

**The Whitespace Column** — Text takes 4 columns on the left. The remaining 8 columns are almost entirely empty, with a single floating detail image in the lower right.

**Staggered Product Row** — Products are presented in a row, but alternating cards are positioned slightly higher or lower, breaking the rigid horizontal line.

### 18.3 Visual Rhythm
No two consecutive sections should use the same layout. The scroll experience must feel like turning the pages of a premium catalogue — each spread is a surprise, but each spread feels unmistakably Avighna.

## 19. Motion Design Language

### 19.1 The Motion Philosophy
Motion at Avighna is earned. Every animation must justify its existence. Our motion language communicates: *thoughtful, deliberate, unhurried confidence.*

### 19.2 Motion Principles

**Principle 1 — Slow is Luxurious**
Standard animation duration: 600ms–1200ms. Nothing faster than 400ms for primary transitions. Fast animations feel cheap.

**Principle 2 — Easing is Character**
We use a single easing curve family: custom cubic-bezier.
```
Primary Ease: cubic-bezier(0.25, 0.1, 0.25, 1.0) — ease — for entries
Exit Ease: cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 1, 1) — ease-in — for exits
Signature Ease: cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) — easeOutExpo — for hero reveals and prestige moments
Spring: stiffness: 100, damping: 30, mass: 0.8 — for physical interactions
```

**Principle 3 — Stagger Creates Hierarchy**
When multiple elements enter simultaneously, they stagger by 80ms–120ms. Never instant simultaneous entry.

**Principle 4 — Enter from Below, Exit Downward**
Content enters from below (y: 30px → y: 0) with opacity 0 → 1. This communicates growth, emergence, and reveal.

**Principle 5 — Parallax with Restraint**
Background images may use 20%–30% parallax offset on scroll. No more. Heavy parallax is gimmicky.

**Principle 6 — Hover is a Whisper**
Hover states are subtle. A 1.02 scale on product images. A hairline gold border appearing. A colour transition of 400ms. Never dramatic hover effects.

### 19.3 Animation Tokens

| Token | Duration | Easing | Usage |
|---|---|---|---|
| `motion-instant` | 150ms | ease | Icon state changes, focus rings |
| `motion-fast` | 300ms | ease | Button hovers, colour transitions |
| `motion-standard` | 500ms | ease | Navigation, drawer open |
| `motion-slow` | 800ms | easeOutExpo | Section reveals, image loads |
| `motion-hero` | 1200ms | easeOutExpo | Hero animations, page transitions |
| `motion-stagger` | 80ms | — | Delay increment for staggered lists |

## 20. Micro-interactions

### 20.1 Navigation
- Logo appears at scroll position 0, then a thin hairline divider appears at scroll > 80px
- Navigation links have a slow-growing underline from left to right (600ms) on hover
- Active navigation link has a permanent 1px gold underline offset by 4px below text

### 20.2 Buttons
- Primary CTA: background opacity transitions from 100% to 90% on hover (500ms)
- A thin gold hairline border expands around the button perimeter on hover (starts from center)
- On press: scale 0.98, returns to 1.0 on release with a spring ease

### 20.3 Product Cards
- Image: gentle 1.03 scale with 800ms ease on hover
- A second product image (detail or alternate angle) fades in with opacity 0 → 1 over 600ms on hover
- Product name: slides upward 4px on card hover (300ms ease)
- Price: appears with opacity 0 → 1 after a 200ms delay when card is hovered
- "Add to Collection" button: rises from the bottom of the card with y: 20px → y: 0 on hover (500ms easeOutExpo)

### 20.4 Cart Interactions
- Cart icon receives a subtle scale pulse (1.0 → 1.15 → 1.0) when item is added (600ms spring)
- Item count badge appears with scale: 0 → 1, opacity: 0 → 1 on first add
- Cart drawer slides in from the right with ease-out, 500ms

### 20.5 Image Galleries
- Gallery transitions: cross-dissolve, 700ms ease
- Thumbnail selection: 3px gold bottom border slides in from left, 300ms ease

### 20.6 Form Fields
- Focus: bottom border animates from 0 to full width in 300ms (the border is `#C9A96E`)
- Error: border transitions to `#8B3A3A` with a subtle shake animation (8px amplitude, 400ms)
- Success: a thin green checkmark fades in to the right of the field

### 20.7 Loading States
- Skeleton screens use a warm shimmer — ivory → warm parchment → ivory, cycling at 1.5s intervals
- No spinning circles or progress bars on product images
- Page transitions: a hairline gold bar progresses from left to right at the top of the viewport

### 20.8 Scroll Reveals
- All above-fold content is visible immediately (no animation)
- Below-fold content reveals on scroll: y: 30px → 0, opacity: 0 → 1, duration: 800ms, stagger: 80ms
- Section headings reveal separately from body content (heading first, 200ms delay, then body)

## 31. Accessibility Principles

1. **Color contrast**: All text meets WCAG AA (minimum 4.5:1 ratio for normal text)
2. **Focus rings**: Visible on all interactive elements (2px gold outline)
3. **ARIA labels**: All icon buttons, form inputs, and non-text interactive elements have descriptive ARIA labels
4. **Keyboard navigation**: Full keyboard navigability — Tab order is logical and predictable
5. **Screen reader**: Product descriptions, prices, and cart interactions are screen-reader compatible
6. **Reduced motion**: All animations respect `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` media query
7. **Alt text**: Every product image has descriptive alt text (e.g., "Kundan bridal necklace set in champagne gold with emerald drops")

---

## 32. Performance Principles

1. **Core Web Vitals first**: LCP < 2.5s, FID < 100ms, CLS < 0.1
2. **Images**: Always use `next/image` with WebP format, appropriate sizes, and `loading="lazy"` for below-fold images
3. **Fonts**: Preload the primary font weights (Cormorant Garamond 400, DM Sans 400, 500) — never load all weights
4. **No unused CSS**: Never import full CSS libraries — scope all styles
5. **Code splitting**: Each page loads only the JavaScript it needs
6. **No cumulative layout shifts**: All images must have defined width/height or use aspect-ratio containers
7. **Animation**: Use `transform` and `opacity` only for animations — never animate `height`, `width`, `margin`, or `padding`

---

## 33. SEO Philosophy

1. **Semantic HTML**: Every page uses proper `h1` (one only), `h2`–`h6` hierarchy, `nav`, `main`, `article`, `section` landmarks
2. **Meta descriptions**: Every page has a unique, compelling 155-character meta description written in brand voice
3. **Product titles**: Follow the pattern: `[Product Name] — Avighna Collections`
4. **Structured data**: All product pages implement `Product` schema with price, availability, and brand
5. **Collection pages**: Implement `BreadcrumbList` schema
6. **Core URL structure**:
   - `/` — Homepage
   - `/collections/[slug]` — Collection pages
   - `/product/[slug]` — Product pages
   - `/shop` — All products
   - `/about` — Brand story
   - `/journal` — Brand journal (future)
   - `/contact` — Contact
7. **No duplicate content**: Canonical tags on all paginated or filtered pages

## 34. Homepage Storytelling Strategy

### 34.1 The Narrative Arc
The homepage is a story told in chapters. Each chapter builds on the previous. The customer is guided through a journey that transforms from stranger to deeply convinced admirer — before being invited to shop.

### 34.2 Chapter Structure

*Goal: Arrest attention, establish brand world*
Full-viewport cinematic image or video. A single headline that names the brand's promise. Two CTAs maximum. No product grid yet. We invite, we don't sell.

**Chapter 2 — The Story (Brand Introduction)**
*Goal: Establish emotional connection*
A short, elegant brand statement in large Cormorant Garamond. Not a product pitch. A belief statement. *"We believe every woman deserves to feel adorned."*

**Chapter 3 — The Collections (Category Navigation)**
*Goal: Orient the customer*
Editorial category tiles — not a standard grid. Asymmetric layout with beautiful photography. Four to five collections maximum. Each tile names the emotion, not just the category.

**Chapter 4 — Featured Collection (Editorial Spotlight)**
*Goal: Create desire for a specific collection*
A full editorial section for the featured collection of the season. This is magazine layout — large image, editorial headline, 2–3 featured products, a clear "Explore" CTA.

**Chapter 5 — The Craft (Brand Values)**
*Goal: Build trust through story*
An editorial text section about craftsmanship. This is not a bullet list. It is prose, in Cormorant Garamond, with a beautiful lifestyle image. It says: *we care deeply about what we make.*

**Chapter 6 — Curated Picks (Products)**
*Goal: Present products in context*
Four to eight products, presented with editorial care. No grid chaos. Staggered layout. Each product feels curated, not catalogued.

**Chapter 7 — The Occasion Story (Gifting/Events)**
*Goal: Expand purchase occasions*
A split editorial — one side about a specific occasion (Wedding, Festival, Gifting). The other side, a curated set of products for that occasion. This teaches the customer how to think about our jewellery.

**Chapter 8 — Trust (Services & Guarantee)**
*Goal: Remove purchase hesitation*
NOT a badge bar. An elegant typography section that names our services as standard privileges: *Complimentary Delivery. Secure Packaging. Satisfaction Guaranteed.* Each service in Cormorant Garamond, separated by gold hairline dividers.

**Chapter 9 — The Journal Tease (Future)**
*Goal: Build brand beyond commerce*
A preview of 2 journal articles. These are brand stories, style guides, cultural essays. They position Avighna as a voice, not just a store.

**Chapter 10 — The Invitation (Newsletter)**
*Goal: Capture the engaged browser*
A full-width, dark background section. A single poetic headline. A single input field and CTA. *"Join the world of Avighna."*

- **Compression**: Under 5MB for hero loops
- **Colour**: Matches brand color grading — warm, champagne-toned

### What Video Should Show
- Jewellery catching light, slowly rotating

## ANTI-PATTERN LIST

*These 75+ patterns must NEVER appear in the Avighna Collections digital experience.*

### LAYOUT ANTI-PATTERNS
1. Standard 4-column Tailwind product grids without editorial composition
2. Bootstrap-style container/row/col layouts
3. Material UI card layouts with rounded corners and shadows
4. Shopify Debut / Dawn template layouts
5. Amazon-style dense product listings
6. Flipkart-style price-first product cards
7. Accordion-heavy page layouts
8. Sidebar + content column layouts (except for filtered shop view)
9. Standard hero with text on left, image on right (without editorial treatment)
10. Full-width background color fills using the gold accent color
11. Boxed website layouts (max-width with visible box border)
12. Tables used for product comparison (use editorial comparison instead)
13. Infinite scroll without editorial breaks
14. Dashboard-style admin layouts on customer-facing pages

### TYPOGRAPHY ANTI-PATTERNS
15. Default browser fonts (Times New Roman, Arial, Helvetica)
16. Google Fonts loaded without font-display: swap
17. Text in all-caps at body size (only for labels, max 13px)
18. Bold headings at display sizes (above 48px, use weight 300–400)
19. Multiple font families (only Cormorant Garamond + DM Sans)
20. Exclamation marks in any product copy or headings
21. Sentence fragments used as section headings without elegance
22. Body text using Cormorant Garamond
23. Headings using DM Sans (except UI-level component headings)
24. Inconsistent letter-spacing — some labels tracked, others not
25. Line heights below 1.6 on body text
26. Text blocks longer than 65 characters per line on desktop

### COLOR ANTI-PATTERNS

29. Pure white (#FFFFFF) as the main page background

# PART VIII — GOLDEN RULES FOR EVERY FUTURE AI SESSION

---

## GOLDEN RULES

*Any AI system, developer, designer, or contributor continuing this project must internalize these rules before touching a single line of code.*

---

### RULE 1 — Read This Document First
Before making any change, read the relevant section of this Design Bible. If the change is not covered by the Design Bible, stop and ask the client before proceeding.

### RULE 2 — You Are Not Building a Website
You are building a digital flagship store. Every decision — from padding to animation timing to copy — must be made with the luxury brand standard in mind, not the standard web development standard.

### RULE 3 — What Can Be Changed
- Page copy (following brand voice guidelines)
- Product data, images, pricing
- Addition of new sections that are explicitly approved
- Bug fixes and performance improvements
- New pages that follow the established design system
- Component variants within the defined design system

### RULE 4 — What Cannot Be Changed Without Explicit Client Approval
- The color palette (no new colors may be introduced)
- The typography system (no new font families)
- The spacing scale (no improvised padding/margin values)
- The motion timing tokens
- The button design language (especially border-radius: 0)
- The navigation structure
- The overall layout philosophy

### RULE 5 — The Restraint Test
Before adding any element, ask: *"Does removing this make the page worse?"* If the honest answer is *"not really"* — remove it.

### RULE 6 — Never Improvise Design
If the design system does not define it — ask before creating it. Do not invent new patterns. Extend the existing system.

### RULE 7 — Mobile First, Always
All new components must be designed mobile-first. If a component does not work beautifully on a 375px viewport, it is not finished.

### RULE 8 — Motion is Earned
Before adding any animation, ask: *"Does this animate because it helps the customer, or because it fills space?"* If the latter — remove it.

### RULE 9 — Copy is a Design Element
Copy is not placeholder text. Every word must be written in brand voice. Never use generic placeholder text ("Lorem ipsum" or "Product description goes here") in any committed code.

### RULE 10 — Preserve Existing Architecture
Do not change folder structure, file naming conventions, import patterns, or component architecture without explicit instruction. Continue from the existing codebase with discipline.

### RULE 11 — How to Review Your Own Work
Before submitting any changes:
1. Run through the Luxury Review Checklist (Part VII)
2. Run through the Anti-Pattern list
3. Ask: *"If I showed this to the Creative Director of Cartier, would they be impressed or embarrassed?"*

### RULE 12 — One Section at a Time
Avighna's development workflow is section-by-section. Never touch unrelated sections. Never refactor working code without approval. Never redesign globally when a targeted improvement is requested.

### RULE 13 — Consistency is Non-Negotiable
A single inconsistency in spacing, color, or typography tells the customer: *"this brand does not pay attention to detail."* Cross-reference every pixel against the design system.

### RULE 14 — The Gold Standard Accent Rule
Champagne Gold (#C9A96E) is used in:
- Section label accents (underlines, before-element ornaments)
- Icon hover states
- CTA hover border
- Decorative dividers (1px hairlines)
- Wishlist heart fill color (use Antique Rose #D4A5A5 instead)
- Active navigation underlines

Gold is NEVER used in:
- Button backgrounds
- Large area fills
- Gradients
- Text headings
- Logo treatment

### RULE 15 — Every Future Session Must Produce a Phase Summary
At the end of every development session, update the walkthrough.md artifact with:
1. What was changed
2. Which sections were touched
3. What the current state of each page is
4. What is next
